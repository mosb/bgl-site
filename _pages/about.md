The **Bayesian Governance Lab** asks how much we can really know about AI systems, and how to act well under what remains uncertain. 
The trajectory of AI is among the most consequential open questions of our moment, and one of the most contested: capable observers disagree about what today's systems can do,
where they are heading, and how they ought to be governed. 
We treat that disagreement as a problem of uncertainty, and uncertainty as something to be measured and reasoned about rather than asserted.

To that end we develop probabilistic machine learning for the assurance, monitoring, and governance of AI systems, giving claims about their behaviour the calibrated confidence that high-stakes decisions demand. 
Our methods draw on Bayesian inference, Gaussian processes, probabilistic numerics, and Bayesian optimisation, applied to the problem of making AI systems trustworthy, auditable, and accountable.

The lab is small by intention. 
We think of ourselves as a fellowship: a close group of researchers held together by a common problem and a shared way of reasoning about it.
We sit within the [Oxford Martin AI Governance Initiative](https://www.oxfordmartin.ox.ac.uk/ai-governance) and keep strong collaborative links across engineering, the social sciences, and policy.

That policy engagement reaches into Parliament and government. 
The lab's lead has given oral evidence to the House of Commons [Science, Innovation and Technology Committee](https://committees.parliament.uk/work/6986/governance-of-artificial-intelligence-ai/) for its inquiry into the governance of AI, setting out the assurance and oversight problems that AI now poses.
This evidence sits within a wider record of advising on the societal effects of AI and automation, from briefings to the Cabinet Office and HM Treasury through to contributions to European policy forums.
